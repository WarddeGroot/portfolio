Enhancing teachers' productivity

Gynzy's icon-only sidebar was confusing teachers and blocking the product from growing. I led a three-phase redesign that cut lesson access from 3 clicks to 1 — and gave the platform room to scale.

My role

UX Designer

The team

Product owner, 3 developers

Duration

± 6 months (3 phases)

The problem

Gynzy's navigation had grown organically over the years into a left sidebar of unlabeled icons. Teachers couldn't figure out what the icons meant. The page they landed on after signing in wasn't even the top icon. Some icons led to features that weren't core to the product at all. And critically, there was zero room left to add new features without degrading the experience further.

Multiple development teams — including ours — recognized the scalability problem independently. Meanwhile, teachers were quietly struggling. This wasn't just a design issue; it was blocking the product from evolving.

Making the case

The data was there, but data alone wasn't enough to convince stakeholders that a full navigation overhaul was worth the investment. So I took a different approach: storytelling. I presented the experience through the eyes of a teacher who had never used Gynzy before — walking stakeholders through what they would see, think, and feel at every step. The confusion of unlabeled icons. The disorientation of landing on the wrong page. The frustration of not finding core features.

This shifted the conversation. Instead of debating whether the navigation was "good enough," we were aligned on the fact that it was actively hurting new and existing users.

The slide deck I used to present the case for overhauling navigation.

Research & exploration

I used Amplitude data to map which features teachers used most and which went largely untouched. Card sorting exercises with educational experts revealed how teachers actually think about their tools — which turned out to be quite different from how we'd organized them. Usability testing with early variations confirmed that simply relabeling the existing icons wouldn't be enough.

A key insight emerged: teachers didn't need a better toolbar. They needed a fundamentally different entry point into the product, and a navigation model that could grow with them.

A phased approach

I quickly realized that the scope was too large for a single release, so I proposed breaking the work into three sequential phases. This let us ship improvements incrementally while building toward the bigger vision.

Phase 1 — Clean up & rebuild

Remove non-core features from the sidebar and rewrite the Ember-based toolbar in Flutter. This was the foundation — reducing clutter and moving to a modern, maintainable codebase. Teachers immediately had fewer distractions, and the team had a solid base to build on.

The toolbar transition from Ember to Flutter — a cleaner, decluttered starting point.

Phase 2 — The Start page

Introduce a dedicated Start page so teachers land somewhere logical when they sign in. Instead of being dropped into a random view, they now see their schedule and most relevant content immediately. This was the single biggest improvement for daily workflow — teachers could open a lesson in one click, down from a minimum of three.

Phase 3 — Secondary navigation

Add a secondary navigation layer to create room for new features without mixing horizontal and vertical navigation patterns. Everything now lives on the left — primary and secondary — giving users a consistent mental model. This unlocked the space the product team needed to ship new capabilities without compromising the experience.

The outcome

The most tangible result: opening a lesson went from at least 3 clicks to just 1 for most teachers. That might sound small, but teachers do this dozens of times a day. Across thousands of users, it adds up to a meaningful shift in how the product feels.

Beyond the numbers, the redesign gave Gynzy a navigation architecture that could actually scale. New features now have a clear home. The mixed Ember/Flutter codebase was consolidated. And the product team went from "we can't add anything" to having room to build.

Perhaps most importantly, I learned that the hardest part of a project like this isn't the design — it's building the conviction to do it properly instead of patching what's there.
